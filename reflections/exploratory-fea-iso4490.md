## Scope

This is an *exploratory FEA portfolio piece* — not a continuation of
the original Uniformity instrument's design history, and not a study
of any Uniformity geometry. I no longer have access to the Uniformity
resources that designed and built the original device. The substrate
for the analysis is the **public ISO 4490:2018 / ASTM B964 funnel
geometry** (60° cone, 2.5 mm orifice for Hall, 5.0 mm for Carney) and
the **published material properties** for AlSi10Mg and 316 SS from
public datasheet and handbook sources.

The frame is "this is what FEA brings to a measurement-instrument
design problem" rather than documentation of any specific instrument.
A hiring manager or any Uniformity reader can verify the inputs
against the public standards and re-run the solvers themselves.

The IP boundary is restated explicitly in
[`../NOTICE.md`](../NOTICE.md) and in the
[`analysis/iso4490-fea/`](../analysis/iso4490-fea/) folder's `README.md`
and `geometry.md`.

## What I built

A pair of pure-numpy 1D linear-FE solvers along the cone slant
coordinate, both with built-in self-verification against closed-form
references:

- **Thermal** — variable-property fin equation along the slant, with
  surface convection on the powder-side and ambient-side faces, and
  insulated boundaries at the orifice and the cup rim. Models the
  steady-state wall temperature of the cone with a 60 °C powder load
  inside and 20 °C still-air ambient outside.
  Verifier: the FE mean wall temperature equals the lumped h-balance
  closed form (`(h_in·T_pow + h_out·T_amb) / (h_in + h_out)`) to
  machine precision when both ends are insulated and the surface BCs
  are uniform along s. Any drift in the verifier output indicates a
  sign error or assembly bug; the verifier raises on failure.

- **Structural** — membrane-shell approximation for a thin conical
  shell under axisymmetric pressure loading from the powder column
  above the orifice. Hoop and meridional stresses follow from the
  classical cone-shell membrane formulae; radial deflection at each
  ring follows from hoop strain.
  Verifier: a uniform-pressure load gives the closed-form deflection
  `w(r) = (1 − ν/2) · p · r² / (E · t · cos α)` at every node; the
  solver matches that node-by-node.

Both solvers run on numpy alone (no SciPy, no FEniCS, no
external mesh tooling), in under a second per matrix on a laptop. The
matrix is two geometries × two materials = four cases per physics.

## What the analysis says

| Mechanism | Δd_orifice | Fraction of ISO 4490 ±25 µm tolerance |
|---|---|---|
| Thermal expansion (60 °C powder, 20 °C ambient) | 1.5–4.0 µm | **6–16%** |
| Gravity load (50 g Hall / 150 g Carney sample column) | < 0.2 µm | **< 0.001%** |

Thermal expansion is the dominant geometric perturbation; gravity load
is several orders of magnitude smaller. The dominant lever for thermal
drift is wall-material CTE — the AlSi10Mg cases drift more than 316 SS
for the same temperature delta, despite Al's higher conductivity (which
flattens the temperature distribution but does not change the mean).

The cone is mechanically over-designed for the 50 g / 150 g powder
columns specified in the standards: hoop stresses peak in the 4–17 kPa
range (vs material yield in the 200 MPa range, safety factor ≫ 10⁴).
This is consistent with the funnel having been spec'd for repeatability
and dimensional stability rather than for structural strength — the
load is trivial.

## What FEA actually buys here

This is the honest portfolio question. For *this* problem, with these
boundary conditions, the FE answer for thermal matches the lumped
h-balance to machine precision; the FE for structural matches a
closed-form ring deflection node-by-node. So why FEA?

Three reasons that generalize beyond this demo:

1. **Spatial resolution when BCs are not uniform.** The insulated-ends
   case used here is the easy case. As soon as the cup is in real
   thermal contact with a fixture or a heat source — or the cone is
   bolted to a base plate at the orifice — the lumped balance breaks
   and only the spatial solver gives the right answer.
2. **Coupling.** Thermal expansion *biases* the structural problem.
   The infrastructure to compose a thermal solve and a structural
   solve is the same infrastructure used here, just with the thermal
   field feeding the structural strain. That coupling is the natural
   next step.
3. **Sensitivity.** Once the solver is parametric, tornado-style
   sensitivity sweeps (e.g. "how does orifice drift change with wall
   thickness, with cone angle, with h_in?") become one-line scripts.
   The lumped formula has the same content but is harder to extend
   when one of the assumptions stops holding.

For an exploratory portfolio piece, the value is the *demonstrated
methodology*, not the headline number — the headline is "the funnel
is well within tolerance under typical conditions," which is
unsurprising. The 1D linear-FE infrastructure with built-in
verification is the durable artifact.

## Limitations

- **Membrane shell, no bending.** Bending modes near the orifice and
  ring stiffeners near the cup rim are smeared by the membrane
  assumption. A proper Donnell or Reissner shell formulation would
  resolve them; for a 1.5 mm wall and 4–17 kPa hoop stress those
  bending corrections are well below the accuracy interesting at
  ISO-tolerance levels.
- **Static powder column.** Real powder bridges and arches; static
  column pressure is an upper bound, not a measured value. Janssen-
  style silo theory would reduce the wall pressure further (powder
  weight is partially supported by wall friction). The static column
  used here is intentionally conservative.
- **Constant convection coefficients.** h_inside and h_outside are
  point estimates from textbook tables (Incropera & DeWitt). Real h
  varies along the slant; for the verifier-matching case (insulated
  ends, uniform h) the wall is isothermal so the variation does not
  bite.
- **Through-thickness isothermal.** Biot number << 1 for both
  materials at the chosen wall thickness; this is well-justified for
  a 1.5 mm steel or aluminum wall at these h coefficients. A thicker
  wall or a powder-side h_inside well above 1000 W/m²·K would invalidate
  the assumption.
- **No ambient radiation, no powder radiation.** Both are < 5 W/m²·K
  at the modeled temperatures; including them would shift the wall
  temperature by < 1 K.
- **Empirical validation deferred.** No instrumented physical funnel
  was tested. A natural L4 follow-on would be a thermocouple array on
  a 3D-printed cone with controlled powder temperature; that is out
  of scope here.

## Reproduce

```bash
cd analysis/iso4490-fea
python3 profile.py          # geometry + material summary
python3 thermal_fea.py      # verifier + thermal matrix summary
python3 structural_fea.py   # verifier + structural matrix summary
python3 run_analysis.py     # writes results.{csv,md} + figures/
```

Pure Python with numpy + matplotlib only. The full analysis (both
physics, four cases each, all figures and CSVs) runs in under three
seconds on a laptop.
